Sichuan hotpot

A dish from Sichuan province in southwest China which is built around a shared pot with bubbling broth together with raw ingredients to add and patience of waiting for your food to cook while talking to good company.

I first ate hotpot in Chengdu, in a small restaurant where the table had a hole cut into its centre for a pot of broth to sit on top of a gas burner. Since I did not understand the list of ingredients that I could order from, I let the waiter choose and a woman eating alone next to me was consulted by the waiter to choose the best things for me. They chose wisely since I liked everything that they brought me.

There is a very special numbing sensation with the Sichuan hotpot which is caused by Sichuan peppercorns that are combined with the heat of chillies and what is called má là (麻辣) in Mandarin. Hotpot is the ultimate dish that combines those two perfectly. Below is a version of the spicy Sichuan broth I have been trying to recreate since I came home, plus a long list of things you can cook in it, feel free to add your own things there. The recipe is for six people.

For the spice paste

  • 1 teaspoon nigella seeds
  • 4 bay leaves
  • 3 dried cloves
  • 9 Sichuan peppercorns
  • 3 dried chillies (more if you want more spice)
  • ½ teaspoon chilli flakes
  • 3 star anise
  • ½ teaspoon coriander seeds
  • 2–3 pieces of cinnamon bark

For the broth

  • Rapeseed oil (enough to cover the bottom of the pot)
  • 2 teaspoons chilli oil
  • 3 garlic cloves, minced
  • 2 thumb-sized pieces of ginger, roughly chopped
  • 2 spring onions, sliced
  • 3 thumb-sized pieces of leek
  • 4 thumb-sized pieces of fresh fennel, plus the green tops
  • 2 tablespoons black bean paste
  • 2 tablespoons Shaoxing wine (or other Chinese cooking wine)
  • 300ml vegetable or beef broth
  • Water, to dilute the broth at the table

For dipping (pick what you like)

Hotpot is very flexible. The principle is that everything goes into the broth raw and cooks at the table, so anything that cooks quickly works. Choose a mix of vegetables and proteins, cut them into pieces small enough to handle with chopsticks and arrange them on plates around the pot.

Vegetables

  • Cauliflower, broken into small florets
  • Celery, sliced
  • Bok choy, leaves separated
  • Water spinach (or regular spinach)
  • Chinese cabbage, cut into wide strips
  • Lotus root, peeled and sliced thin
  • Potatoes, sliced thin
  • Sweet potato, sliced thin
  • Daikon radish, sliced
  • Water chestnuts
  • Pumpkin or winter squash, sliced thin
  • Corn on the cob, cut into chunks

Mushrooms

  • Enoki mushrooms (separated into small bunches)
  • Shiitake mushrooms, whole or halved
  • Oyster mushrooms, torn into pieces
  • King oyster mushrooms, sliced
  • Wood ear mushrooms (soaked first)
  • Button mushrooms, halved

Proteins

  • Thinly sliced raw beef (sirloin or rib-eye, sliced paper-thin, usually available in Asian supermarkets)
  • Thinly sliced raw lamb
  • Thinly sliced raw pork belly
  • Chicken, cut into bite-sized pieces
  • Shrimp, shelled
  • White fish fillets, cut into chunks
  • Squid or cuttlefish, cut into rings
  • Fish balls or meatballs (from an Asian supermarket)
  • Chinese sausage (lap cheong), sliced

Tofu and soy

  • Firm tofu, cut into cubes
  • Fried tofu puffs
  • Soy sheets or tofu skin (soaked first)
  • Fermented bean curd (a small amount, for richness)

Starches

  • Glass noodles (mung bean noodles)
  • Sweet potato noodles
  • Udon or other wheat noodles
  • Rice noodles
  • Wonton wrappers or small dumplings

For the dipping sauce

  • 6 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 2 tablespoons Chinese black vinegar (Chinkiang vinegar)
  • 2 tablespoons sesame oil
  • 1 teaspoon ground coriander
  • ½ teaspoon paprika powder
  • Optional: chopped fresh coriander, minced garlic, chilli oil, sesame paste

A note on the sauce: Every region in China and every family has their own hotpot dipping sauce. The version above is a starting point and was given to me by a Chinese friend from Shanghai. In Sichuan, sesame oil is often used by itself or with garlic and coriander to cool the spicy broth. In northern China, sesame paste is more common. Mix to your own taste.

Method

  1. Grind all the spice paste ingredients together in a blender or mortar until they are a fine powder.
  2. Cut all your dipping vegetables, proteins and tofu into bite-sized pieces. Slice meats and root vegetables very thin so they cook quickly in the broth. Arrange everything on plates that will fit around the pot.
  3. Cover the bottom of a wide pot with rapeseed oil and heat over medium-high heat. Add the chilli oil, minced garlic, chopped ginger, spring onions, leek and fennel. Let everything brown lightly for a few minutes.
  4. Add the ground spice paste and stir for thirty seconds until fragrant.
  5. Add the black bean paste and Shaoxing wine. Stir to combine.
  6. Pour in 300ml of vegetable or beef broth. Bring to a simmer and let it cook gently for 20 minutes to let the flavours combine.
  7. Begin adding water, about 100ml at a time, letting the broth return to a boil between each addition. Continue until you have enough broth to fill the pot two-thirds full.
  8. Transfer the pot to a portable burner or hotplate at the centre of the dining table. Keep it at a gentle simmer throughout the meal.
  9. Mix the dipping sauce ingredients in a small bowl and divide into individual sauce bowls for each person.
  10. Each person adds whatever they want to the pot, waits for it to cook (most things take 1–3 minutes), fishes their pieces out with chopsticks or a slotted spoon, dips them in their sauce and eats. Refill the pot with water or broth as needed throughout the evening.

A few notes

Hotpot is not a recipe to rush. The whole point of the meal is that the pot stays bubbling at the centre of the table for as long as the company wants to keep eating. Plan on at least two hours, three is even better. Have plenty of cold drinks (or milk) on hand since the the broth gets hotter as the evening progresses and the chillies steep.

Cook the meats last. Vegetables release liquid and aromatics into the broth as they cook, which makes the flavour better for everything that follows. Leafy greens and mushrooms cook quickly, the root vegetables and potatoes need a few minutes longer and thin-sliced meats are usually done within thirty seconds.

If you have leftover broth, strain it and freeze it. It makes an extraordinary base for noodle soup the next day.

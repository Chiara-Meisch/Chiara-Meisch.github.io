Why English and Korean speakers make different mistakes in Chinese

A new study shows that our mother tongue shapes how we learn foreign languages, sometimes helping us along and sometimes getting in the way.

Anyone who has learned a foreign language knows that some rules come easily, while others stay difficult even after years of practice. A new study by the linguist Jiajia Su now shows why and that the answer has a lot to do with the language we already speak.

Su examined how native English and Korean speakers learn the Chinese plural marker -men. This small suffix, similar in some ways to the German -en in Katzen, follows its own clear rules in Chinese: it can only be used for people, not for animals or objects and only when referring to specific, identifiable individuals.

What is surprising is that although both English and Korean have plural forms of their own, the two groups of learners made completely different mistakes when learning Chinese.

When the mother tongue helps and when it gets in the way

The Korean learners had an advantage. Their mother tongue also distinguishes between definite and indefinite plural forms and they quickly understood when -men should be used. But they struggled with grammatical rules that work differently in Korean, especially the way -men interacts with numerals.

The native English speakers showed the opposite pattern. They first had to learn the distinction between definite and indefinite plural forms, which English does not mark in the same way. But once they had understood it, they had fewer problems with the grammatical restrictions on where -men can appear.

The plural marker -men
  • Can only be used for people, not animals or objects.
  • Refers to specific, identifiable individuals.
  • Interacts in complex ways with numerals, which might be a source of difficulty for learners.

How our brain organises languages

Su argues that these results support the Feature Reassembly Hypothesis. This theory supposes that when we learn a foreign language, we begin by mapping the grammatical patterns of our mother tongue onto the new language. Only over time do we reassemble these mental structures to fit the language we are trying to learn.

The good news is that, in the end, all of the learners in Su's study were able to acquire the rules of the Chinese plural marker, even if the path was different depending on where they started from. Advanced learners showed almost no differences from native speakers at all.

Why this matters for language teaching

The findings have a practical implication for language teaching. Teachers should be aware that learners coming from different language backgrounds will face different challenges, even when they are learning the same rule. A one-size-fits-all approach falls short. A Korean speaker and an English speaker walking into the same Chinese class are learning slightly different things.

Reference Su, J. (2022). L2 acquisition of the Chinese plural marker -men by English and Korean speakers. Linguistic Approaches to Bilingualism. https://doi.org/10.1075/lab.21025.su

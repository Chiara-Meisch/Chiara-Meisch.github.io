Shantaram

[One-sentence summary of what you took from the book, or a quote from the book, or a single specific claim about it.]

Gregory David Roberts’ book Shantaram takes place in Bombay, now called Mumbai, and covers love, violence, friendship, loss and moral grey areas. What makes the book so interesting is its unpredictability. Every chapter takes the story in a direction you do not expect which made it difficult to put down. It explores the entire spectrum of human nature from kindness to brutality and shows what people are capable of. As the protagonist slowly falls in love with Mumbai and its people, the reader does too. At its heart, the book is about trying to understand life while already living it, the way other people shape our thoughts and identity and the beauty of human encounters.

[Heading]

One of the novel’s strengths is the way it introduces the reader to Maharashtrian culture and life in Mumbai. As the protagonist gradually discovers the culture for himself, the reader learns alongside him. The increasing use of Hindi, Urdu and Marathi throughout the novel shows his integration into Indian society. At the beginning, none of these languages appear but by the end almost every conversation contains words or expressions from them. It is a journey into Indian culture and the languages themselves. The protagonist’s willingness to learn and speak the local languages is portrayed as a sign of respect and the reactions of the people around him reflect this. Once the obviously foreign protagonist speaks their language, people immediately light up and that says everything about respect and what it means to meet people where they are.

This same sense of openness shows in the way the characters are introduced. Almost everyone is described without prejudice and with an almost childlike innocence that assumes goodness in people until proven otherwise. Instead of telling a story through its characters, the characters are the story. Every character is written with a complexity that makes them inseparable from the narrative itself. Their pain, losses, victories and struggles feel real and each experience leaves the reader reflecting on their own life and choices, even while being immersed in a world that may feel unfamiliar. Yet the novel never tries to teach lessons directly. But just by the way of storytelling, the observations come naturally through the experiences of the characters.

[Heading]

Although the author clearly has a strong love for Mumbai, he never romanticises the city and instead portrays its beauty and hardness in equal measure. The people living in poverty are described not through pity, but through their personalities, motivations and dignity. They are shown as resourceful, generous and capable of remarkable things when they work together. In doing so, the novel suggests that circumstances do not determine character and that human connection and solidarity can exist even in the harshest conditions.

[Heading]

The novel’s refusal to judge its characters directly is also what gives it much of its philosophical depth. It explores moral grey areas and suggests that people sometimes do terrible things for reasons they believe are justified. This raises difficult ethical questions about what can truly be considered “bad.” If a harmful act ultimately leads to something good, can it still be condemned as evil? That ambiguity runs through the entire book. The violence and suffering are described with a calm and reflective neutrality and even the protagonist’s worst experiences are written in a way that considers the perspective of everyone involved. As a result, the novel restores a sense of faith in humanity despite the darkness it portrays.

However, one criticism of the novel, is its portrayal of women. The story is mainly about male experiences, which partly reflects the worlds the protagonist moves through but it also means that female characters are often filtered entirely through his perspective. For the protagonist, women tend to become emotional anchors or love interests instead of fully realised individuals in their own right. Karla is the clearest example of this because she is presented as an almost mythological figure and beautiful enigma placed on a pedestal rather than a person with her own history and contradictions. Whether this shows the limitation of the novel itself or simply the flawed and romanticised worldview of the protagonist remains an open question.
